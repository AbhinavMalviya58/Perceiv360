Heinz guderian : A Remarkable man in terrible times

by Ayush Pandey 05-JAN-2023

Heinz Wilhelm Guderian ( 17 June 1888- 14 May 1954) was a general in the German army who is popularly known among the historians as “Father of Blitzkrieg” for his contributions to the technique of modern day warfare centralized along light armoured tanks. He can be best described as “A remarkable man in terrible times”.

General Heinz Guderian

First of all let us understand Blitzkreig. It is a form of warfare in which the attacking army cuts deep through enemy defenses bypassing enemy strongholds, concentrating their all strength at a single point instead of fighting on a wide frontier using fast moving armoured vehicles encircling and pocketing large number of enemy troops spreading incoordination and confusion making them raw meat for the following infantry to mop them up.

German Panzers used during WWII for Blitzkrieg

Guderian was one of the biggest and earliest pioneers and improvers of armoured warfare who revolutionized the tank warfare to a new next level. On his part he was a true nationalist who at all cost wanted to contribute to the glory and defend the integrity of his motherland. He also had a certain moral code of conduct which was unique in its own way. Despite being the central piece of Germany’s early success in WWII , he clearly refused to participate or contribute anyhow to the Nazi atrocities on jews. He would often argue with his superiors as his juniors. He was one of the very few who would not only express his disagreements but would fiercely argue with the Fuehrer. He was a good leader but never really possessed the ability to be a blind follower.

Heinz was a junior officer in the German army and was in his early stages of training in a command center which majorly focused on improving radio coordination between ground troops in the battlefield when the WWI broke out . He after completing his training was designated as a staff officer during the war itself. After the war there was a discontent in the army officers and they believed that they did not lose the war in the battlefield but due to the cowardness of their witless politicians which smoothened the rise of fascists in Germany and Hitler was the face of their leadership.

During the war Guderian was impressed by the effectiveness of the way the British tanks drove German army backwards deep in the countryline and pocketed large number of German troops disrupting co-ordination He extensively read the works of Lidell Hart and Fuller and was really taken into believe that evolving and using light tanks in a radically new way will prove effective in the upcoming wars. Guderian came up with a simple ideology – “ if you want to hit someone in the face, you hit him with your fist not with your fingers widespread” .

A after being promoted to the rank of Major in 1927, Guderian was put in charge of motor units but his primary work there was to deal with logistics and transport vehicles. In an attempt to modify machine warfare Guderian put some machine guns on fast moving vehicles but was immediately stopped and opposed by his superiors. His real nourishing and exposure came in 1929 Kazan visit when he was sent to a secret tank testing facility developed in partnership with the Russians and the Weimar Republic. There he freely put forward his ideas and exchanged experiences with his Russian counterparts who had little idea they would be on the south side of the battlefield against him.

In 1933, Guderian was made colonel in the German army. His articles and research works have gained attention of top military thinkers and leaders throughout the world including the leader of Nazi party that was swiftly rising to power. When Hitler started rearmament Guderian was put incharge of the Panzer division.

In Early 1934 when Hitler was demonstrated with the Panzer division, Hitler was quite amused with their ability to swiftly cut across long distance. Guderian recalls Hitler's reaction as " the chancellor was much taken by the speed and precision of movement of our units and said repeatedly ' That's what I need !' ". This demonstration made Hitler believe in doctrine of Guderian and from then he provided every possible support to Guderian until the outbreak of the war. With overwhelming support from the Fuhrer himself Guderian was already punching above his weight and that made him somehow unpopular in the eyes of his superiors. This difference would later surface on the battlefield when the superiors did not agree with Guderian's plans and it would cost the German army very dearly.

The first real demonstration of Blitzkrieg came when Germany invaded Poland on September 01, 1939. The Pols were not only outnumbered but they were also very primitive with cavalry brigades riding on the backs of horses and lances. Britain and France declared war on Germany in support of Poland on Sept 03, 1939 but by that time Poland was encircled and a series of surrenders was already underway. The German panzers cut deep through Polish territories with two major pincer movements one aimed to meet near Warsaw (the Polish capital ) and the outer one at Brest-Litovsk .Unlike Germany the Polish army dispersed its tanks to support its infantry which was already stretched defending the long Polish borders which made very less number of tanks at a point and were of no consequential effect. The skies were dominated by the new breed German Airforce “ Luftwaffe” whose strength seemed unmatchable by its rivals due to its new breed of Stuka Dive bombers and it completely wiped out the Polish airforce within first 2 days of the battle. The Pols fought bravely but were no match for their German counterparts . Legend even has it that some Polish cavalry charged on the German panzers on their horsebacks but were brutally brushed aside. The Polish campaign was successfully accomplished within a month of its start with last two weeks majorly spent conquering Warsaw that offered stiff resistance.

The success of the Polish campaign made Hitler believe in Guderian’s Blitzkreig warfare . But this was just the start of the series of grand success that this form will provide Germany in the first two years of the war.
